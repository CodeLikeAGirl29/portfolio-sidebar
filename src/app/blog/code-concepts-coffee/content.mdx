Studying for the Florida Real Estate License is an intense process. You have to master a mountain of information spanning property law, complex finance formulas, and detailed appraisal methods.

After reviewing my practice scorecards and realizing I needed a more active way to drill the material, my frontend developer brain kicked in. I didn't just want a better study tool; I wanted to _build_ it.

That is how my current project, **Florida Real Estate Drill**, was born.

## Why Build Instead of Just Read?

There is a concept in learning called "The Generation Effect," which suggests that information is better remembered if it is actively generated from your own mind rather than passively read.

By designing the database structure for real estate laws and programming the logic to calculate amortization schedules, I am forcing myself to understand the material on a fundamental level. You can't write a TypeScript function to calculate a property's cap rate unless you truly understand how the math works.

## The Tech Stack

I wanted this app to be fast, modern, and highly interactive. Here is the stack I am currently using to bring it to life:

- **Next.js 16 (with Turbopack):** For the core framework. The routing is intuitive, and the speed of Turbopack makes the local development experience incredibly smooth.
- **TypeScript:** Real estate involves a lot of strict data types (percentages, currency, specific legal statuses). TypeScript keeps my code honest and catches errors before they ever hit the browser.
- **Tailwind CSS:** Because studying shouldn't be ugly. Tailwind allows me to rapidly prototype clean, distraction-free interfaces so I can focus on the questions, not the clutter.

## The Challenges of State Management and Scorecards

One of the most interesting technical hurdles so far has been building the scorecard system.

When you take a 100-question practice exam, the application needs to track your progress in real-time, allow you to flag questions for later review, and then generate a comprehensive breakdown of your strong and weak areas (e.g., scoring 90% in Finance, but 60% in Law).

Managing that complex, nested state across different components without causing massive performance bottlenecks has been a fantastic exercise in React architecture.

## Two Birds, One App

Building the Florida Real Estate Drill platform is the ultimate "two birds, one stone" project.

On one hand, it serves as a robust, practical addition to my developer portfolio, showcasing my ability to take a complex real-world problem and architect a full-stack solution. On the other hand, it is the exact tool I need to finally conquer those tricky appraisal concepts and pass the exam.

If you are a developer trying to learn a complex new subject, I highly recommend trying to code a tool for it. You might be surprised by how much you learn along the way.
